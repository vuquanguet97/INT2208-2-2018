Vintage Shop

There is instant fashion cred that comes with owning a Jean Paul Gaultier piece. Over the course of nearly four decades, the irreverent French designer has forged his own visual language, built on graphic patchwork prints and a subversion of sex and gender norms not seen elsewhere. Fans recognize his work on sight and, more often than not, become rabid collectors—chief among them, Collin James Weber and Brandon Veloria Giordano of James Veloria, the cult Chinatown vintage shop that will hold an epic Gaultier archival sale this week at Opening Ceremony.


Last fall, the couple sat down for dinner at Kiki’s Greek Tavern in Chinatown and received a warm greeting from Opening Ceremony cofounder Humberto Leon, who was seated nearby. Over a bowl of avgolemono, a hearty egg and lemon soup, Leon suggested the pair hold a vintage pop-up in his Soho store. “Gaultier was a natural choice for us both,” Weber says. “We’re all about helping people live their fashion fantasies, and we feel Gaultier really does that best!”



Both Weber and Giordano fondly recall their first piece of JPG. Giordano saved up for a skintight sailor set in high school with eye hooks running up the back of the pants (“I wore them in my small town in Ohio, and there was no looking back for me”), and Weber by chance thrifted a jacket with long bondage straps extending from the collar from Fall 1993’s controversial Chic Rabbis collection. The joyful liberation and self-expression they found in his clothes had them hooked. “One day you can be a Times Square cowboy, and the next, an Op Art alien,” Giordano says. “He’s not one for minimalism and subtlety and neither are we or OC—life is too short!”


Highlights include a Fall 1989 cage corset jumpsuit (“such iconic Gaultier,” Giordano says) and a floral and graffiti-print men’s dress from Spring 1995 that Weber loves for its disruption: “He didn’t worry about following the traditional rules in fashion and was way ahead of his time in that way,” he explains.
